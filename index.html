Research

Biomedical Imaging analysis group

We are a tranlational team between the University of Barcelona and the Hospital Clinic of Barcelona. Our research focuses on the application of structural and functional imaging techniques -mainly Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) and Positron Emission Tomography (PET)- to study human brain in health and disease. We perform our own research projects and we provide technical support to interdisciplinary research, where the use of advanced image processing methods becomes a useful tool towards the understanding of cognitive neuroscience and its clinical applications. In the field of PET-imaging, our research is based on improving methods for quantification, assessing various methodological choices such as the correction for Partial Volume Effects or the use of different brain regions as reference and target for quantifying PET uptake. Furthermore, using MRI-related techniques, our work is centered on the study of the structure and function of brain networks.

    Roser Sala-Llonch

    roser.sala@ub.edu

    Roser Sala Llonch is graduated in Telecommunication Engineering from the Polytechnic University of Catalonia (2008). Later, she obtained an MSc (2010) and a PhD (2015) in Neurosciences at the University of Barcelona. She developed her PhD at the Department of Psychiatry and Clinical Psychology of the University of Barcelona, under the supervision of Dr Carme Junqué Plaja and Dr David Bartrés Faz. During her PhD she did a 6-month internship at the Center for Functional MRI of the brain (FMRIB), at the University of Oxford. She defended her thesis entitled "Multimodal MRI study of human brain connectivity: cognitive networks", in January 2015. During this period, she also collaborated in many research projects related to the use of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) techniques to investigate brain function and structure in the healthy brain and in a variety of brain diseases. During the years 2015 and 2016, she worked as a postdoctoral researcher at the "Center for Lifespan Changes in Brain and Cognition" (LCBC), from the University of Oslo, Norway. There, she participated in several neuroimaging studies related to the discovering of novel biomarkers for the study of healthy aging and the detection and monitoring of Alzheimer's disease. In trying to keep a balance between methodological research and clinical applications, her work always covers a wide range of topics, from the basic methodological and technical investigations to the clinical applications. Since October 2016, she holds a position as an Assistant Professor at the Department of Biomedicine at the University of Barcelona, where she performs teaching and research tasks. Since her incorporation, she has been working with the within the Biomedical Image group of the University of Barcelona (GIB-UB), whit Dr. Javier Pavía and Dr. Domènec Ros ag group leaders. In this context, she got expertise in Positron Emission Tomography (PET) and nuclear medicine techniques. Her current research is related to the use of MRI and PET to the study of brain function and structure. She has ongoing collaborations with several clinical groups, including the Nuclear Medicine Service and the Neurology Services from the Hospital Clinic de Barcelona and the Hospital de Sant Pau, and the Barcelonaßeta brain research center. In addition, she actively collaborates with the Center for Functional MRI of the brain (FMRIB), at the University of Oxford, where she participates in developing tools for the FSL software, which is one of the most widely used for neuroimaging studies. She is part of the Editorial Board of The Journal of Alzheimer's Disease and member of the Organization for Human Brain Mapping society.

    Final Degree Project

    Title: Measuring Cortical Thickness with MRI in a transgenic animal model of AD.

    Supervisors: Raúl Tudela and Roser Sala-Llonch

    Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is a progressive age-related neurodegenerative disorder, which can be studied through the use of transgenic animal models. AD is characterized by a loss of brain gray matter, resulting in changes in the cortical thickness that can be measured from Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI). The present work aimed to measure the cortical thickness from rat brain MRI by creating an automated tailored program implemented in Python and using available algorithms and in-house designed functions. The program was further used to analyse the temporal evolution of the cortical thickness in a transgenic AD model compared to control wild-type rats.

    Final Degree Project

    Title: Quantification of PET-Amyloid images for improving classification in Alzheimer’s disease

    Supervisor: Roser Sala-Llonch

    The present thesis contains the development of a protocol for Positron Emission Tomography(PET) image processing, one of the neuroimaging techniques used in the study of Alzheimer’sDisease (AD). The project has been developed using data from the Alzheimer’s DiseaseNeuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), an international, multi-centered online database. The selecteddataset included Cognitively Normal (CN) subjects as well as patients at different disease stages,such as Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) and AD.One of the main goals of the study was to improve quantification methods. The results haveshown how proper quantification can be achieved by using the cerebellum as a reference regionwhen assessing the Standardized Uptake Values for the different brain regions. The mostinformative Regions of Interest (ROI) for studying AD were found by using statistical analysistechniques, such as t-tests between the different stages, and they were the left and rightprecuneus, left and right bankssts, left and right putamen, frontal and temporal.Furthermore, classification algorithms have been used to create a prediction system through machine learning, that can assess the disease state of a patient. In conclusion, we have developed a pipeline for PET image processing of AD patients and a classification algorithm based on data retrieved from the worldwide ADNI database. Further improvements could include using anatomical information as well, creating an interface for theuser and increasing the variety of outcomes in the prediction system.
